# Uncharted Journeys - Encounter Design Guide

This document serves as a reference for creating new regions that match the writing style and design patterns of the official Uncharted Journeys book.

## Region Structure

Each region in Uncharted Journeys follows this structure:

### 1. Region Introduction (1 paragraph)
- Sets the tone and atmosphere of the region
- Describes the defining characteristics (terrain, climate, culture)
- Hints at the types of peoples and dangers found there
- Example (Frontiers): "These are the borders where settled lands meet the untamed wilds; a last familiar place before plunging into the unknown. These regions are characterised by their great expanse, wide vistas and big skies; often swampy or dusty, they are shaped by the other regions they stand between."

### 2. Flavor Tables (10 entries each)
- **Weather**: Atmospheric conditions from pleasant to catastrophic
- **Flora and Fauna**: Wildlife and plant life characteristic of the region
- **Local Inhabitants**: NPCs and communities the party might encounter
- **Points of Interest**: Landmarks and locations worth visiting
- **Possible Journeys**: Adventure hooks and quest ideas

### 3. Encounter Tables (12 types, 10 entries each = 120 encounters per region)

## Encounter Type Analysis

### A Chance Meeting
**Purpose**: Social encounters with NPCs - travelers, merchants, pilgrims, or unusual individuals
**Tone**: Generally neutral to friendly; opportunities for roleplay, information gathering, or aid
**Design Patterns**:
- Introduce a memorable NPC with a distinctive trait or profession
- Present a situation or need that invites interaction
- Leave room for the party to help, trade, or simply converse
- Often includes hooks to other adventures

**Writing Style**:
- Opens with vivid description of the NPC or situation
- Uses present tense for immediacy
- Names specific creature types when appropriate (e.g., "Lizardfolk", "Dragonborn")
- Often ends with an open-ended opportunity

**Example Titles**: "Circuit Judge", "Grace for Sale", "First Contact", "Pony Express", "Map Swap"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #9 - Map Swap):
> "The party encounters a Half-Orc pathfinder who is trying to help a neighbouring nation establish a fort along the frontier. He is willing to share his map and insights with the party in return for a look at theirs. He tells them that the fort captain is looking for a party to scout the area beyond the fort's immediate territory."

---

### Hidden Reserves
**Purpose**: Resource discovery - supplies, treasure, or useful locations
**Tone**: Mysterious, often with a twist or dark undertone
**Design Patterns**:
- Present an abandoned or unusual location
- Describe what can be found/salvaged
- Include a mystery about why it's there or what happened
- Sometimes hint at danger that has passed (or lingers)

**Writing Style**:
- Emphasizes discovery and exploration
- Includes specific loot or benefit descriptions
- Often poses questions that create atmosphere
- May reference skill checks for additional discoveries

**Example Titles**: "Abandoned Mining Camp", "Airship Wreckage", "Icy Mystery", "Reanimation Ranch", "Liquid Gold"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #7 - Reanimation Ranch):
> "The party come across a bustling farm, but on closer inspection, every worker is either a Zombie or a Skeleton. If they investigate, the farm owner is indeed a Necromancer, who claims to be trying to aid the community with 'efficient and basically free labour.' She offers the party as much produce as they can carry if they can help to improve her reputation with the neighbouring farmers."

---

### A Bump in the Road
**Purpose**: Obstacles requiring the Outrider's skills to overcome
**Tone**: Challenging but surmountable; tests navigation and survival
**Design Patterns**:
- Present a terrain or situation blocking progress
- Specify which skill check(s) can resolve it
- Describe consequences of failure (usually delays or minor harm)
- Often involves natural hazards or territorial conflicts

**Writing Style**:
- Opens with the obstacle description
- Names the specific skill check required (e.g., "Strength (Athletics)", "Wisdom (Survival)")
- Describes what success and failure look like
- Uses the Outrider as the key role

**Example Titles**: "River Ford", "Swampland", "Bovine Blockade", "The Holy Pass", "Secret Quicksand"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #4 - The Holy Pass):
> "A community at the mouth of a mountain pass believes that the pass leads to the home of the gods. They allow no one to enter the pass except to accompany funeral processions that leave bodies on a 'sacred ridge' not far from the village. An Intelligence (Religion) check or a Charisma (Deception) check by the Outrider can convince the community that the party should be allowed to pass."

---

### Needing Assistance
**Purpose**: NPCs requiring help - group skill challenges
**Tone**: Sympathetic; appeals to heroism and problem-solving
**Design Patterns**:
- Introduce someone in distress or with a problem
- Describe the situation requiring group effort
- Specify group skill checks that can help
- Reward is often goodwill, information, or items

**Writing Style**:
- Focus on the NPC's need or predicament
- "A group [Skill] check" format for resolution
- Often presents multiple skill options
- Emotional hooks (grieving widow, lost child, struggling community)

**Example Titles**: "Buried Them Deep", "Wounded Soldier", "Trapped!", "Party Foals", "Dead and Unburied"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #6 - Dead and Unburied):
> "A grieving woman approaches the party. Her recently deceased partner is banned from being buried in the town's cemetery for being a Wererat. The group can help her in one of these ways: a Carpenter's Tools check to create a beautiful casket, a Strength (Athletics) check to dig a fresh grave, or a Charisma (Intimidation) check to convince the town to bury her beloved."

---

### Danger Afoot
**Purpose**: Combat encounters or immediate threats
**Tone**: Tense, dangerous; clear antagonists
**Design Patterns**:
- Introduce the threat and its source
- Name specific monster stat blocks to use
- Describe the setup and potential tactics
- Often includes environmental elements

**Writing Style**:
- **Bold** creature names (in the book) - use for stat block references
- Active, urgent language
- Describes monster behavior and motivation
- Often includes a twist (allies aren't what they seem, trap is set)

**Example Titles**: "Lone Stranger", "Unwelcome Refuge", "Predator/Prey", "Ghost Stories", "Tumbleweed Terror"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #2 - Unwelcome Refuge):
> "After a tiring day, the Outrider spots a small hunting cottage in the distance. It looks inviting enough, a sturdy building that can protect them from both the elements and predators. Unfortunately, the front door is actually a Mimic lying in wait, with another Mimic disguised as a rocking chair on the porch."

---

### Natural Wonders
**Purpose**: Awe-inspiring locations or phenomena
**Tone**: Wonder, mystery, sometimes dread; emphasizes the fantastic
**Design Patterns**:
- Describe something visually spectacular or impossible
- May include magical properties or effects
- Often tied to legends or local lore
- Can be beautiful, terrifying, or both

**Writing Style**:
- Lyrical, evocative descriptions
- Emphasizes sensory details (sight, sound, smell)
- Often poses mysteries or legends
- May hint at adventure opportunities

**Example Titles**: "Stark Truth Beneath the Stars", "Crystal Canyon", "Giant Golf", "Dusty Dreams", "Eternal Honey"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #9 - Swamp of Resizing):
> "Crimson red bubbles rise into the sky from a sulphurous, ever-bubbling blood-red swamp. Animals regularly drink from its waters, and all that do randomly increase in size. If the party linger here, they will observe a Swarm of Rats drink from the oozing pool and become 20 Giant Rats."

---

### Monster Hunt
**Purpose**: Tracking and fighting a specific monster threat
**Tone**: Hunter becomes hunted; investigation leading to combat
**Design Patterns**:
- Establish the monster's presence through signs/rumors
- Describe local impact and why it must be dealt with
- Name specific monster stat blocks
- Include treasure or reward motivation

**Writing Style**:
- Often starts with local warnings or evidence
- **Bold** monster names for stat blocks
- Describes the creature's lair or hunting grounds
- Often includes a reward from grateful locals

**Example Titles**: "Giants Revealed", "Fiery Domain", "The Pristine Forest", "Missing Persons", "Lost Labyrinth"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #4 - Missing Persons):
> "People and livestock in a frontier village have been going missing one by one each night, causing mass panic. A Phase Spider is the culprit, snatching villagers and dragging their webbed bodies back to its hungry children for future consumption. The sheriff will pay a handsome reward to those who can squash the spider."

---

### A Place to Rest
**Purpose**: Safe havens with character and potential complications
**Tone**: Refuge with a twist; not always what it seems
**Design Patterns**:
- Describe a location suitable for resting
- Introduce interesting inhabitants or features
- Include a potential complication or surprise
- Often leads to roleplay opportunities

**Writing Style**:
- Initially welcoming descriptions
- Introduces memorable NPCs or unusual circumstances
- Often hints at secrets or hidden dangers
- May reference other encounter types for follow-up

**Example Titles**: "The Old Fleet", "The Giant Homestead", "Monk Hospitality", "Hagspitality", "Tiny Home"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #5 - Hagspitality):
> "Dancing lights in the darkness lead the party to a welcoming cottage with an unruly garden. The proprietor is a friendly older female Commoner with a half-dozen Cats, a Raven, and several Frogs as pets. She exclaims how delighted she is to have guests and immediately offers them freshly baked cookies and tea, though strangers aren't always to be trusted."

---

### Old Memories
**Purpose**: Ruins, remnants, and echoes of the past
**Tone**: Melancholic, mysterious; history made tangible
**Design Patterns**:
- Describe abandoned or ancient locations
- Include clues about what happened here
- May include hidden treasures or secrets
- Often features skill checks for discovery

**Writing Style**:
- Nostalgic, contemplative tone
- Describes decay and abandonment
- Includes specific details that tell a story
- Often poses unanswered questions

**Example Titles**: "Poor Soil", "The Wall", "Halfling Memories", "Forgotten Monument", "The Old Haunt"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #5 - Just Right):
> "A home, long abandoned, holds three of everything: beds, chairs, bowls, and more. The name on the door reads 'BjÃ¶rnstad' along with a welcome mat reading 'Wipe Your Paws'. The lock has been broken by a previous intruder and it appears the family left in a hurry. An Intelligence (Investigation) check reveals strands of golden hair and blood stains."

---

### A Dark Place
**Purpose**: Disturbing, creepy, or evil locations
**Tone**: Horror, unease, dread; something is very wrong here
**Design Patterns**:
- Establish atmosphere of wrongness immediately
- Include visceral, disturbing details
- May feature undead, curses, or dark magic
- Danger is often ambiguous or lurking

**Writing Style**:
- Gothic, unsettling descriptions
- Uses death imagery and dark themes
- May include skill checks to understand the horror
- Often leaves mysteries unresolved

**Example Titles**: "The Grinning Witch", "Cursed Road", "Strange Bumps", "Village Slaughter", "The Church"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #10 - The Church):
> "An old, boarded-up church sits on a hill. It is inhabited by Wights who once refused to fight and sought refuge here. They were cursed by their god for their cowardice and refused entry into the afterlife until they can prove themselves worthy. They tell their tale solemnly to the party, still unwilling to draw blood, despite their curse."

---

### Deadly Fight
**Purpose**: Serious combat encounters with high stakes
**Tone**: Life-threatening; enemies are dangerous and motivated
**Design Patterns**:
- Establish clear threat and antagonist motivation
- Name powerful monster stat blocks
- Include environmental or tactical elements
- Often connected to previous events or region themes

**Writing Style**:
- Urgent, action-oriented language
- **Bold** monster names
- Describes enemy tactics and numbers
- Often includes consequences for the region

**Example Titles**: "Supper Interrupted", "The Secret Threat", "Meet the Deity", "Ghost Riders", "Firebrand"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #5 - Ghost Riders):
> "The howling wind disguises the sounds of screaming. The hunt of the infamous ghost riders (Wraiths on Nightmares) has begun. They travel the winds, killing the weak and inducting the strong into their eternal hunt across the plains."

---

### Fateful Encounter
**Purpose**: Plot-significant meetings with lasting consequences
**Tone**: Mysterious, significant; hints at larger stories
**Design Patterns**:
- Introduce an NPC or situation with deeper implications
- Include moral complexity or difficult choices
- Often features powerful beings or important figures
- Consequences extend beyond the immediate encounter

**Writing Style**:
- Intriguing, plot-hook focused
- Names specific creature types including celestials/fiends
- Describes hidden truths and revelations
- Often ends with unresolved tension

**Example Titles**: "Dual Plagues", "Wolf's Clothing", "Opposites Attract", "Strange Omens", "Low Noon"

**Full Example** (Frontiers #4 - Opposites Attract):
> "A town of Humans is unusually run by two mayors: a Bearded Devil and a Deva. This balance of good and evil keeps the town neutral, and strict agreements are in place to ensure no violence between the angel and devil, though a secret love is blossoming between the two."

## Writing Conventions

### Creature References
- Use **bold** for creature names that reference stat blocks
- Examples: **Mimic**, **Gnolls**, **Young Red Dragon**, **Werewolf**
- For generic NPCs, don't bold: "a priest", "bandits", "townsfolk"

### Skill Check Format
- Format: "Skill (Specialization)" - e.g., "Intelligence (Investigation)", "Charisma (Persuasion)"
- Group checks: "A group [Skill] check"
- Contested checks: "a contested [Skill] check"

### Title Style
- 2-4 words, evocative and memorable
- Often uses wordplay, alliteration, or references
- Should hint at content without spoiling
- Examples: "Liquid Gold", "Ghost Riders", "Fool's Gold", "Hagspitality"

### Description Length
- 3-5 sentences typical
- Complete narrative arc: setup, situation, opportunity/resolution
- Enough detail to run, flexible enough to adapt

### Tone Consistency
- Match the encounter type's expected tone
- Frontiers: rugged, pioneering, lawless, opportunity-filled
- Each region has its own flavor that should permeate all encounters

## Frontiers-Specific Themes

### Core Identity
- Edge of civilization, where law is scarce
- Pioneer spirit, independence, self-reliance
- Diverse peoples seeking fresh starts
- Nature pushing back against settlement
- Mix of hope and danger

### Common Elements
- Abandoned settlements and failed ventures
- Gold rushes and land claims
- Religious movements and new faiths
- Frontier justice (circuit judges, vigilantes)
- Communication challenges (isolated communities)
- Monster encroachment from the wilds
- Cultural clashes with indigenous peoples
- Resourceful survivors and con artists

### Creature Palette
- Dragons (especially young/territorial)
- Gnolls, Centaurs, Giants
- Undead (Zombies, Skeletons, Wights, Wraiths)
- Mimics, Oozes, Basilisks
- Nightmares, Werewolves, Oni
- Giant animals (Lizards, Frogs, Toads)

## Detailed Frontiers Analysis (Based on Pages 120-133)

### A Chance Meeting - Frontiers Patterns
**Professions Encountered**: Circuit judges, itinerant priests/con artists, herbalists, caravan members, pioneers, religious converts, postal riders, pathfinders/scouts, amnesiacs
**Common Hooks**:
- Person fleeing danger (First Contact)
- Hidden agenda (Dangerous Herbs - stolen dragon egg)
- Request for help (Wagons Retreat)
- Information exchange (Map Swap)
- Mystery about identity (Amnesiac)

### Hidden Reserves - Frontiers Patterns
**Location Types**: Mining camps, crashed airships, ice formations, abandoned villages, flooded mines, ranches, farms, riverbanks, swamps
**Salvage vs. Mystery Balance**: Each location offers both resources AND a story/question
- "What scared the miners away?"
- "Why is there ice in warm weather?"
- "Why would a Necromancer farm openly?"

### A Bump in the Road - Frontiers Patterns
**Obstacle Types**: Natural (floods, swamps, quicksand, hills), Cultural (holy places, Tiefling territory, dueling towns), Creature (cattle herds)
**Skill Check Variety**: Athletics, Survival, Animal Handling, Religion, Deception, Persuasion, Intimidation, Sleight of Hand, Investigation, Stealth
**Regional Flavor**: Frontier-specific obstacles like dueling competitions, religious frontier communities, persecuted peoples seeking refuge

### Needing Assistance - Frontiers Patterns
**Problem Types**: Mysterious sounds (graverobbing), medical emergencies, lost children, outdated maps, lost livestock (Centaur foals), social prejudice (Wererat burial), escaped creatures with dreams, impractical inventions, confused trolls, land disputes
**Resolution Options**: Always multiple skill check options per encounter
**Emotional Range**: From whimsical (Giant Frog musician) to tragic (grieving widow)

### Danger Afoot - Frontiers Patterns
**Threat Sources**: Individual aggressors, mimics, fleeing creatures bringing worse danger, awakened guardians, undead survivors, illusory traps, devil cults, transformed bandits, desperate veterans, victorious hostiles
**Twist Patterns**:
- What seems safe is dangerous (Unwelcome Refuge - Mimic door)
- Ally becomes threat (Ghost Stories - cannibal Halflings)
- Illusion reveals ambush (Too Good to be True)
- Third party conflict spills over (Move Along)

### Natural Wonders - Frontiers Patterns
**Wonder Types**: Cosmic (starry skies), Wildlife (vast herds), Geological (crystal canyons, volcanoes, stone arches), Atmospheric (dust storms), Magical (inverted sewage waterfall, resizing swamp), Creature-maintained (bee hive towers, petrified farm)
**Tone Balance**: Mix of sublime beauty and grotesque wonder
**Player Agency**: Some are purely atmospheric, others offer interaction opportunities

### Monster Hunt - Frontiers Patterns
**Monster Variety**: Giant Apes, Young Red Dragon, Treants, Phase Spider, undead T-Rex, Kobold + Giant Wolf Spider, Minotaur, Magmins, Giant Toad, Werebear
**Setup Patterns**:
- Warning signs before reveal (messages about "giants")
- Environmental evidence (charred forest, red scales)
- Local lore prohibition (pristine forest)
- Missing persons pattern
- Archaeological discovery gone wrong
**Reward Integration**: Treasure from previous victims, grateful locals, magical items

### A Place to Rest - Frontiers Patterns
**Haven Types**: Repurposed structures (ships as homes, giant homesteads), Institutions (monasteries), Individual hosts (airship captains, mysterious old women), Natural formations (graveyard tunnels), New communities, Magical locations (shrinking boots), Archaeological camps, Suspicious camps
**Complication Types**: Cultural unfamiliarity, size mismatches, requests for sparring, mysterious origins, too-good-to-be-true hospitality, bored mercenaries, hidden criminality

### Old Memories - Frontiers Patterns
**Ruin Types**: Failed farms, mysterious walls, abandoned villages (Halfling, Duergar), forgotten temples, haunted taverns, failed wells, collapsed caverns
**Story Elements**: Each ruin tells a complete mini-story through environmental details
**Discovery Opportunities**: Skill checks reveal hidden information or treasures
**Atmosphere**: Melancholy over lost hopes and abandoned dreams - very frontier appropriate

### A Dark Place - Frontiers Patterns
**Horror Elements**: Mass death sites, cursed roads with bird corpses, ritual animal sacrifice, zombie villages with patterned bodies, Mousefolk genocide, abandoned entertainment halls with clockwork children, empty altars with drained victims, fungal body horror, fresh warning totems, cursed cowardly Wights
**Tone Escalation**: Each entry more disturbing than simple danger - existential dread, moral corruption, mysteries of evil

### Deadly Fight - Frontiers Patterns
**Combat Scenarios**: Predator pursuit (Manticore), shapeshifter infiltration (Oni), corrupt community (dragon worship + poison), werewolf revenge, undead cavalry (Ghost Riders), ooze in mine (Black Pudding), demonic incursion (Dretches), mounted bandits (Nightmares), monster siege (Gnolls + Giant Scorpions), geological hazard + ambush (Grick)
**Stakes**: Often entire communities at risk, not just the party
**Connection to Other Encounters**: Often callbacks to previous encounter types

### Fateful Encounter - Frontiers Patterns
**Moral Complexity**:
- Both sides have legitimate grievances (Dual Plagues - Goblins also suffering)
- Protective acts appear threatening (Wolf's Clothing - demon wards look evil)
- Party member's fate written in prophecy (Strange Face)
- Good/Evil balance maintained by love (Opposites Attract)
**Revelations**: Hidden identities (Rakshasa noble, Changeling son, time-displaced Elf), secret parentage (Pit Fiend + Planetar), protective sacrifices (Warlock sparing children)
**Long-term Hooks**: Many encounters set up ongoing storylines
